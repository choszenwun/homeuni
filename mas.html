The Male Attenuation Spectrum (MAS)

A clinical taxonomy describing the compounding set of chemical, pharmacological, surgical, and cultural forces that systematically suppress male biology, psychosexual function, and autonomic regulation.

Definition

Overview

The Male Attenuation Spectrum (MAS) identifies and classifies the documented vectors through which male endocrine function, neurological integrity, sexual capacity, and autonomic regulation are degraded. These vectors operate across four domains: chemical, pharmacological, surgical, and cultural.

MAS is not a single condition. It is a convergence model. Each vector compounds the others. A man exposed to xenoestrogens, prescribed a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor, circumcised at birth, and culturally conditioned toward chronic sympathetic dominance is not experiencing four separate problems — he is inside a compounding suppression cascade.

The overarching term for this convergence is The Suppression Axis.

Compounding

The Convergence Effect

MAS vectors do not operate independently. They stack. The clinical significance of MAS is in the compounding — each vector lowers the threshold at which subsequent vectors become pathological.

A man circumcised at birth has an autonomic system primed toward freeze-state vulnerability. Exposure to xenoestrogens degrades his endocrine baseline. An SSRI prescription suppresses his serotonergic-sexual axis. Finasteride depletes his neurosteroids. Cultural conditioning normalises chronic stress, suppresses parasympathetic recovery, and substitutes genuine intimacy with dopaminergic surrogates.

No single vector may be sufficient to produce clinical pathology. The convergence is the pathology.

Suppression Axis

The Suppression Axis is the term for the aggregate effect of MAS vectors operating simultaneously on a single organism. It describes a state of compounding biological suppression that is greater than the sum of its individual components.
